Strategic Report for the Year Ended 30 June 2020

The directors present their strategic report for the year ended 30 June 2020.

Fair review of the business

The results for the year to 30 June 2020, which are set out in the profit and loss account, show turnover of £27,240,615 (2019 - £32,702,529) and a pre-tax loss of £4,346,090 (2019 - £5,315).

At 30 June 2020 the Group had tangible fixed assets valued in the financial statements at £19,654,959 (2019 - £19,836,285) and total assets less current liabilities of £17,824,542 (2019 - £18,596,205).

We had an excellent start to the year with strong like for like growth and a successful new opening in the first 8 months of the year. However, the spread of Covid-19 which resulted in a mandatory Government lock down of much of the leisure and tourism sector on 20 March 2020, meant that we were unable to trade until the end of our financial year, over 3 months. This clearly impacted the final result for the year.

We had anticipated a lock down, and the inevitable loss of revenue, and took proactive steps. Prior to lock down all our employees agreed to a 20% reduction in pay/ or hours. We entered negotiations with all landlords and in most cases were able to agree deferred rent terms, as well as rent free holidays. We also ceased all payments to HMRC, and over the following months agreed deferred payment terms.

The Government offered significant assistance, and the speed in which the initiatives were established, and then delivered has been of tremendous benefit. The ability to access the furlough scheme has been fundamental to this. Given the complete shutdown of the business we were able to furlough over 95% of our workforce. Through proactive communication we ensured that we kept the team engaged, and loyal to our business, which has really benefited us as we reopened. In addition to the furlough scheme we benefited from the rates holiday given to the leisure sector, providing immediate and sustained support.

We also immediately put a stop to all major capital projects, and thanks to our strong supplier relationships we have been able to do this without any significant incremental cost to the business.

In addition to these actions we have the benefit of a strong financial position. We entered the crises with healthy cash reserves, having been able to access existing facilities where we had a good level of headroom. However, the nature of the pandemic means that at no point could we be complacent. With the support of our debt and equity providers post year end we have been able to increase our facilities. In addition we have simplified our covenants, with just one cash based covenant test until December 2021. We also extended the term of our facilities by a further 2 years until November 2025, giving us significant stability for the years ahead.

Collectively the measures we have taken have given the business significant stability, and as a board we were confident that we could weather a prolonged lockdown.

Fortunately we were able to reopen on the 4th July. However, we took a considered approach, and opened our tracks in phases.

During the period of lockdown we developed a detailed opening strategy, with the safety of our staff and our customers at the heart of everything we did. We engaged with local EHO officers to seek guidance on our plans, we reduced capacity at our sites to ensure social distancing both on and off track, and invested in specialist cleaning equipment across our estate.

We are pleased with the progress made, but we recognise that we face a volatile period ahead, with no one approach fitting every potential outcome. Despite this, we remain confident in our ability to manage what is an evolving situation on an almost daily basis.

In the short term we do expect further disruption. However, we are grateful to have a loyal customer base that has helped underpin our success. In addition, our ability to offer a safe environment, when other operators in the leisure sector are not able to operate, provides a significant sales opportunity.

Future developments

We expect the next 12 months to remain volatile. We have sadly seen a number of independent operators permanently close, but we are confident that we have the financial strength to continue to operate successfully. The Directors remain committed to delivering exceptional service, in a COVID-secure environment.

Financial instruments

Going concern

Financial instruments
Classification
Financial instruments are classified and accounted for according to the substance of the contractual arrangement, as financial assets, financial liabilities or equity instruments. An equity instrument is any contract that evidences a residual interest in the assets of the company after deducting all of its liabilities. Where shares are issued, any component that creates a financial liability of the company is presented as a liability on the balance sheet. The corresponding dividends relating to the liability component are charged as interest expenses in the profit and loss account.

Recognition and measurement
All financial assets and liabilities are initially measured at transaction price (including transaction costs), except for those financial assets classified as at fair value through profit or loss, which are initially measured at fair value (which is normally the transaction price excluding transaction costs), unless the arrangement constitutes a financing transaction. If an arrangement constitutes a financing transaction, the financial asset or financial liability is measured at the present value of the future payments discounted at a market rate of interest for a similar debt instrument.

Financial assets and liabilities are only offset in the balance sheet when, and only when, there exists a legally enforceable right to set off the recognised amounts and the company intends either to settle on a net basis, or to realise the asset and settle the liability simultaneously.
 

Impairment
Assets, other than those measured at fair value, are assessed for indicators of impairment at each balance sheet date. If there is objective evidence of impairment, an impairment loss is recognised in the profit or loss account as described below.

Non-financial assets:
An asset is impaired where there is objective evidence that, as a result of one or more events that occurred after initial recognition, the estimated recoverable value of the asset has been reduced. The recoverable amount of an asset is the higher of its fair value less costs to sell and its value in use.

Where indicators exist for a decrease in impairment loss, the prior impairment loss is tested to determine reversal. An impairment loss is reversed on an individual impaired asset to the extent that the revised recoverable value does not lead to a revised carrying amount higher than the carrying value had no impairment been recognised. Where a reversal of impairment occurs in respect of a CGU, the reversal is applied first to the assets (other than goodwill) of the CGU on a pro-rata basis and then to any goodwill allocated to that CGU.

Financial assets:
For financial assets carried at amortised cost, the amount of an impairment is the difference between the asset’s carrying amount and the present value of estimated future cash flows, discounted at the financial asset’s original effective interest rate.

For financial assets carried at cost less impairment, the impairment loss is the difference between the asset’s carrying amount and the best estimate of the amount that would be received for the asset if it were to be sold at the reporting date.

Where indicators exist for a decrease in impairment loss, and the decrease can be related objectively to an event occurring after the impairment was recognised, the prior impairment loss is tested to determine reversal. An impairment loss is reversed on an individual impaired financial asset to the extent that the revised recoverable value does not lead to a revised carrying amount higher than the carrying value had no impairment been recognised.
